Generator Maintenance Checklist Kenya 2026 — 20 Point Inspection Guide

By Okumu Jared — Electrical Supervisor, UNMISS June 25, 2026 6 min read

In my 11 years as an Electrical Supervisor — including my current role with UNMISS (United Nations Mission in South Sudan) where I oversee fifteen 500 kVA diesel generators — I have seen generators fail in almost every possible way. And in nearly every case, the failure was preventable.

Most generator breakdowns in Kenya come down to one thing: no structured maintenance checklist. Technicians do what they remember. They miss what they forget. A KES 200,000 engine overhaul happens because someone skipped a KES 500 oil change.

This guide gives you the exact 20-point inspection checklist I use on UN mission-critical generator systems. Use it monthly and your generator will run reliably for years.

💡 Field rule: A generator that is never serviced will fail at the worst possible moment — during a blackout, during surgery, during a critical IT backup. Maintenance is not optional. It is insurance.

Why Generator Maintenance Fails in Kenya

Before the checklist, understand the three reasons maintenance fails in most Kenyan facilities:

  1. No written schedule — maintenance depends on one person's memory. When that person leaves, the knowledge goes with them.
  2. Reactive not preventive — teams only act when something breaks. By then, minor damage has become major damage.
  3. No documentation — without records, you cannot track patterns, predict failures, or prove compliance to auditors.

A written checklist solves all three problems. It creates a system that works regardless of who is on duty.

The 20-Point Generator Maintenance Checklist

Run this inspection every month. Document every result.

🔋 Engine Checks (Daily / Weekly)

  1. Engine oil level — check dipstick, top up if below minimum mark. Use manufacturer-specified grade.
  2. Coolant level — check overflow tank and radiator. Never open a hot radiator cap.
  3. Fuel level and fuel quality — check for water contamination and algae growth especially in tanks unused for weeks.
  4. Air filter condition — inspect for dust blockage. A blocked air filter starves the engine of oxygen and wastes fuel.
  5. Battery voltage — should read 12.4V minimum at rest. A weak battery causes failure to start when you need it most.
  6. Battery terminals — check for corrosion, tighten connections, apply terminal grease.

⚡ Electrical Checks (Monthly)

  1. Output voltage — measure at the alternator terminals. Should be within ±5% of rated voltage (e.g. 415V ± 20V for 3-phase).
  2. Output frequency — should read 50Hz ±1Hz. Frequency drift indicates governor problems.
  3. Earth/ground connection — test continuity from generator frame to earth rod. A missing earth is a fatal electrocution risk.
  4. Circuit breaker condition — operate each breaker manually. Check for heat discolouration which indicates overload history.
  5. ATS (Automatic Transfer Switch) — test automatic changeover from mains to generator and back. Time the transfer — should be under 10 seconds.
  6. Control panel alarms — test each alarm: high temperature, low oil pressure, overcrank. These are your early warning system.

🔧 Mechanical Checks (Monthly)

  1. Belt tension and condition — fan belt and alternator belt should have 10–15mm deflection. Cracked or glazed belts must be replaced immediately.
  2. Hoses and clamps — inspect all coolant and fuel hoses for cracks, soft spots, or leaks. Tighten all clamps.
  3. Exhaust system — check for leaks at joints and manifold. Carbon streaks indicate a leak. Exhaust gases in an enclosed space are fatal.
  4. Mounting bolts and anti-vibration mounts — loose mounts cause cracks in fuel and coolant lines over time.

📋 Load and Performance Checks (Monthly)

  1. Load test — run generator at 75–100% load for 30 minutes minimum. A generator that only ever runs on light load develops wet stacking — unburned fuel deposits in the exhaust.
  2. Oil pressure at running speed — read the oil pressure gauge at normal operating temperature. Compare to manufacturer specification.
  3. Operating temperature — coolant temperature should stabilise within the normal range. Overheating means a blocked radiator, low coolant, or a failing thermostat.
  4. Record all readings in a maintenance logbook — date, readings, technician name, any defects found and actions taken. This logbook is your compliance record and your diagnostic history.
📋 Pro tip from the field: On UN mission sites we log every generator reading in SAP/CMMS. But even a simple paper logbook or Excel sheet works. What matters is consistency — same checks, same time, every month, documented every time.

When to Call a Professional

Some issues require a qualified Electrical Supervisor or engineer:

  • Voltage fluctuating more than ±10% under load
  • Generator hunting (engine speed surging up and down)
  • Black or white smoke from exhaust under normal load
  • Any alarm that cannot be reset after correcting the obvious cause
  • Unusual knocking or rattling from the engine
  • ATS failing to transfer automatically

Do not attempt alternator repairs or governor adjustments without the correct training and test equipment. The cost of a qualified technician is always less than the cost of a damaged alternator winding.

Summary

A diesel generator is a significant asset — often worth KES 500,000 to KES 5,000,000 or more. Protecting that asset costs almost nothing compared to replacing it. Twenty checks, once a month, documented every time. That is the difference between a generator that lasts 20 years and one that fails at 5.
